Claims & Evidence
CLAIM 01 · Market Definition

Mid-market is 250 kWh – 5 MWh, behind or in front of distribution feeders

The report defines mid-market as projects between 250 kWh and 5 MWh of useable energy capacity, sited behind a commercial or industrial meter or in front of a distribution feeder serving 50–5,000 customers. This is the fastest-growing segment of the storage market.

→ p.3 §1 Market Structure
CLAIM 02 · Growth Trajectory

Segment grows ~3.7× from 2.1 GWh to 7.8 GWh by 2028

Driven by soft-cost collapse rather than cell price decline, the mid-market segment is projected to nearly quadruple in three years, while the median project size falls from 1.9 MWh to 1.1 MWh as developers move down-market.

→ p.2 §Executive Summary
CLAIM 03 · Cost Structure

Soft costs are the dominant lever; LFP price decline is secondary

LFP cell price decline has decelerated to 6.4% YoY. The real structural change is software-driven compression of interconnection engineering, permitting, and commissioning — the "software-eatable layer" — now deployed by 3 of 4 surveyed developers.

→ p.4 §2 Cost Structure
CLAIM 04 · DERMS Architectures

Three DERMS models split the market: vendor-locked (40%), SaaS (35%), operator-built (~25%)

Vendor-locked OEM dispatch is reliable but slow to adapt. Independent SaaS is fastest to respond to new market mechanisms (e.g. ERCOT Ancillary Tier 3) but introduces contractual complexity. Operator-built retains the most margin but requires the highest engineering investment.

→ p.5 §3.1 Three Architectures
CLAIM 05 · Key Bottleneck

Interconnection queues, not capital, are the binding constraint

Front-of-meter pricing alone justifies a four-hour battery on every modelled site. The remaining barrier is utility interconnection queue backlogs. Reform of queue processes is the single highest-leverage policy action for accelerating mid-market deployment.

→ p.6 §4.1 Interconnection Reform Stalls
CLAIM 06 · Risks

Four counter-theses: queue reform stalls, cell tariff volatility, insurance capacity, model uncertainty

The report identifies interconnection reform stalling, surprise cell tariff changes, constrained battery insurance capacity, and acknowledged model uncertainty as the primary risks that could invalidate the bullish growth thesis for mid-market storage through 2028.

Glossary · p.8
DERMS
Distributed Energy Resource Management System — software sitting between battery hardware and market dispatch signals.
LFP
Lithium Iron Phosphate — the dominant cell chemistry for stationary storage at this scale.
Behind-the-meter (BTM)
Battery sited on the customer side of the utility meter; revenue from demand-charge avoidance and self-consumption.
Front-of-meter (FTM)
Battery sited on the grid side of the meter; revenue from wholesale market participation.
Interconnection queue
The utility study process projects must pass before connecting to the grid — identified as the primary bottleneck.
Soft costs
Non-hardware project costs: permitting, engineering, interconnection studies, commissioning.
Mid-market
Projects between 250 kWh and 5 MWh useable capacity — the segment this report analyses.
IRR
Internal Rate of Return — the primary financial metric used to rank portfolio sites.
