Chapter 1 — Affordability

How Much House Can You Actually Afford?

Two numbers matter more than your maximum loan amount. Sharon walks through both — and shows why the bank's answer and your answer should never be the same.

The Reframe

The bank will tell you one number. That number is not your budget.

Almost every first-time buyer walks into the process with the same question: "What's the maximum I can borrow?" It's a reasonable place to start — you need an upper bound before you can tour anything. But here's what Sharon sees happen, client after client: people treat the maximum loan amount as a target, not a ceiling. They find the house that hits their ceiling, fall in love with it, stretch to close, and then spend the next several years feeling the financial equivalent of wearing shoes one size too small.

The bank isn't calibrated to your life. It doesn't know that you're planning to have a second child in 18 months. It doesn't know that your parents are aging and you might need to cover $3,000 a month in care costs by 2027. It doesn't know that you value two international trips a year and a fully-funded 529. The bank's number is built on two inputs: your income and your existing debt. It is a legal limit, not a quality-of-life recommendation.

So what are the two numbers that actually matter?

Number One
①
Your comfortable monthly payment
The monthly all-in housing cost (principal + interest + property tax + insurance + HOA) that lets you still save, still live, still breathe. This is a lifestyle number, not a math number. Work it out before you talk to any lender.
Number Two
②
Your downpayment + reserves cushion
The cash you can actually deploy — downpayment, closing costs (typically 2.5–3%), and six months of mortgage payments held in reserve after closing. Many buyers have enough for a down but not the full picture.

Sharon's rule of thumb: if your target monthly mortgage payment is more than 32% of pre-tax monthly household income, you're going to feel it every month for 30 years. Not every month is a vacation, a car repair, or a medical bill — but some months are all three. The 32% threshold is where financial stress starts to compound. In the Bay Area, where property taxes run 1.1–1.25%, HOA fees in many Cupertino communities exceed $500/month, and median sale prices for a 4-bedroom single-family home sit near $3.2M, that threshold matters more, not less.

"The shortlist surfaces from 8–12 tours, not the top few. But the shortlist only works if you already know what you can absorb. Walk in with your number, not the bank's number."

— Sharon Wang, on every first buyer consultation

Underwriting Reality

What Lenders Actually Look At

The pre-approval process feels like a black box, but there are really five variables that drive the outcome. Understanding them before you apply lets you correct anything correctable — and stops you from wasting a hard credit inquiry on a file that isn't ready.

📊
1 — Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI)
Lenders calculate two DTIs: "front-end" (housing costs ÷ gross income, cap 28–31%) and "back-end" (all monthly debt ÷ gross income, cap 36–43% for conventional loans, up to 50% for FHA). Your car payment, student loans, and minimum credit card payments all count. A $600/month car lease can eliminate $100K in home-buying power at 6.75%.
Sharon "Before submitting any offer, Sharon pulls an updated copy of your liabilities. Buyers routinely forget that a co-signed auto loan or a deferred student loan shows up on your DTI even if you're not the primary payer."
🎯
2 — FICO Score
Conventional loans typically require a minimum 620, but to get the best pricing tier you want 740+. The spread between a 680 score and a 760 score on a $1.5M loan can be 0.4–0.6% in rate, which at 6.75% baseline translates to roughly $350–$550 more per month for 30 years. Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion are all pulled; lenders use the middle score of all borrowers' middle scores.
Sharon "Don't open a new credit card, lease a car, or close an old card in the 90 days before applying. All three move your score in ways that take months to recover."
🏢
3 — Employment History
Lenders want 24 months of stable, documentable income. W-2 employees at Apple, Google, or Meta are straightforward — two years of pay stubs plus an offer letter if you changed jobs recently. Self-employed borrowers and contractors need two years of tax returns, and lenders use the average net income after expenses, not revenue. RSUs are only counted if they've been received and are likely to continue for at least 3 more years.
Sharon "If you changed jobs in the last 6 months within the same field and at higher pay, most lenders are fine. If you went from W-2 to 1099, plan to wait 18–24 months before applying — or use a bank statement loan at a higher rate."
💰
4 — Downpayment Source
Every dollar of your downpayment must be "sourced and seasoned." Sourced means you can document where it came from — brokerage account, HYSA, savings, 401(k) withdrawal, or a gift letter from a family member (gifts must be documented separately). Seasoned means it's been in your account long enough that underwriters aren't worried about undisclosed loans. Typically 60 days of bank statements are required to show the funds sitting there.
Sharon "Large cash deposits or transfers in the 60 days before application get flagged. If a parent is contributing, do the transfer now — not during the week you're writing offers."
🛡️
5 — Reserves After Closing
Most conventional lenders want to see 2–6 months of housing payments remaining in liquid accounts after you close. For jumbo loans above $766,550 — which covers nearly every purchase in Cupertino, Los Altos, or Palo Alto — lenders often require 12 months of reserves. This is not money you spend on the downpayment; it has to be left in your accounts after the wire goes out.
Sharon "Reserves are where strong income earners get tripped up most often. They've been saving for the downpayment and used nearly everything. The calculator above shows you this number explicitly — run it honestly."
Myth vs. Reality

The 20% Downpayment Myth

There is a persistent belief — often from parents who bought in a different era — that you need exactly 20% down to buy a home. That is not true. You can buy with 3% down (Fannie Mae HomeReady), 3.5% down (FHA), 5% down (conventional), or 10% down (many jumbo programs). The 20% threshold exists for a specific reason: it's the point at which Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) drops off automatically.

PMI on a $1.8M purchase price at 10% down typically runs $0.4–0.6% annually on the loan balance — roughly $650–$950 per month that disappears once you hit 20% equity, either through appreciation or additional principal payments. On top of PMI, going under 20% on a jumbo loan (which most Bay Area purchases are) often means a slightly higher rate — usually 0.125–0.25% above the conforming rate, adding another $200–$400/month on a $1.5M loan. And with a smaller equity cushion, a 5–8% market correction can push you briefly underwater, which matters if you need to sell within 3–5 years.

Sharon's threshold is straightforward: "If you can hit 15%, go to 15. If 20% means tapping your 401(k) or leaving yourself with less than 3 months of reserves, stop at 15." The PMI cost is real but calculable. The cost of a depleted emergency fund when your HVAC fails in August, or when one partner's RSU vest gets delayed, is not calculable in advance — but it's always larger than you expect.

💡
One more thing about the 20% number: in California, property values have appreciated at an average of 5.2% annually over the last 20 years in the Cupertino–Sunnyvale corridor. A buyer who waits an extra two years to save the last 5% of a downpayment often finds that the target property has risen by more than their additional savings. Sharon doesn't give investment advice — but she does show you the math when you ask.
Real Numbers, Anonymized

A Walk-Through: $320K Income, $250K Saved. Which House?

One of Sharon's clients in late 2024 — call them the Chens — came in pre-approved for $2.6M. They had a $320,000 household income (two engineers, one at Nvidia, one at Apple), $250,000 in savings earmarked for a downpayment, and a toddler starting kindergarten in Cupertino Union School District (API 945) in 2027. They'd been looking at 4-bedrooms in the $2M–$2.6M range in Cupertino and Sunnyvale. The question was simple: should they push to $2.6M for the extra space and a corner lot, or hold at the lower end?

Sharon's reasoning on the $2.1M target: Even at $2.1M, the Chens are at 59% housing-to-gross — well above the 32% comfortable ceiling. The recommendation is $2.1M not because it's comfortable in the traditional sense, but because the two-engineer income is stable, the downside risk is bounded (both have senior titles at stable public companies), and the Cupertino USD boundary requirement eliminates lower-priced options anyway. The critical constraint is reserves: at $2.6M they close with effectively zero liquidity. Sharon required that the Chens have an additional $80K in untouched emergency reserves before submitting any offer above $2.1M. That condition was met four months after the initial consultation, and the final offer was submitted at $2.18M — within their calibrated range, and below the appraisal.

*The $2.1M scenario also leaves reserves near zero. Sharon's recommendation included a holding period of 4 months to build the $80K buffer. This is the "90-day close path" in practice — the timeline is set by financial readiness, not by desire.
