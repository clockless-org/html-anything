📊 Credit & Financing ⏱ 8 min read

The 760 / 740 / 720 / 680 Ladder

Most buyers think of credit as binary — "good" or "bad." Lenders don't. They price your mortgage rate in precise 20-point FICO bands. On a $2M jumbo loan in 2026, each 20-point drop costs you approximately 0.15% in rate. That sounds minor. It is not.

The 760→720 spread: ~$42,000 in additional lifetime interest on a $2M loan. Moving from your current 720 to 740 saves an estimated $21,000 over 30 years and — more practically — may improve your debt-to-income ratio enough to qualify for the better jumbo tier. That alone is worth spending two months on.

Why Lenders Weight FICO So Heavily

It's not arbitrary. FICO is the single most predictive variable lenders have found for 90-day default risk. Every point on the score represents a statistically derived probability that you'll stop paying. When a jumbo lender is putting $1.6M of their capital on the line for 30 years, they are extremely sensitive to that probability curve.

Here's the part most buyers miss: in the jumbo tier (loans above $1,149,825 in most Bay Area counties in 2026), you're not dealing with Fannie Mae's automated underwriting. You're dealing with the lender's own portfolio rules. Many of Sharon's preferred jumbo lenders in Santa Clara County have a hard tier break at 740. Below that, they either add a rate premium, require a larger reserve (think 24 months PITI rather than 12), or both.

The secondary factor is how FICO interacts with loan-to-value (LTV). At 80% LTV — which is exactly where you'd be on a $1.8M purchase with 20% down — a 720 score combined with high utilization can trigger what underwriters call a "risk layering" flag. It doesn't kill the loan. It slows it down, adds conditions, and occasionally kicks it to manual review. In a Cupertino seller's market where a 21-day close is competitive, a 45-day underwriting extension can cost you the house.

The 3 Fastest Moves to Lift Your Score in 90 Days

These aren't guesses. These are the levers Sharon's clients have pulled, validated with their lenders, and confirmed with score updates. Ranked by impact-to-effort ratio.

  1. 1
    Pay revolving balances down to under 30% utilization — aim for under 10% if possible

    Credit utilization (how much of your available revolving credit you're using) accounts for 30% of your FICO score and it's the fastest-moving variable. Unlike payment history or credit age, utilization is recalculated fresh every billing cycle. If you can pay down one or two cards significantly before the statement closes, your score will reflect it within 30–45 days. The biggest bang comes from getting any single card below 30%, then targeting below 10% for the highest-utilization card. Don't spread the paydown evenly — attack the highest utilization account first.

    Score impact visible in 30–45 days
  2. 2
    Do not close old credit lines — length of history is 15% of your score

    This is the most common mistake Sharon sees. A client pays off an old card, feels good about it, closes the account. Two things happen simultaneously: their available credit drops (utilization goes up) and their average account age decreases. Both hurt the score. If you have a card you haven't used in three years, leave it open with a $0 balance — or put one recurring subscription on it and set up autopay. The account keeps aging, the utilization stays near zero, and you preserve 15% of the scoring model that's working quietly in your favor.

    Closing an old card can drop score 15–30 pts
  3. 3
    Pull all three bureau reports and dispute any inaccurate hard inquiries or duplicate accounts

    Go to annualcreditreport.com (the only federally mandated free source). Pull Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion separately. Look specifically for: (a) hard inquiries you don't recognize — these account for 10% of score and stay for 2 years, (b) accounts listed as open that you've closed, (c) any balance discrepancies versus your actual statements. A successful dispute removes inaccurate hard inquiries within 30–45 days and can add 10–20 points depending on how many you remove. One client of Sharon's found a hard pull from a car dealership they'd visited two years earlier — three inquiries in one day for "rate shopping" they'd never authorized. Removing them moved the score from 731 to 749.

    Dispute resolved in 30–45 days by law

What Sharon Tells Her Clients About Timing

Sharon Wang
Buyer's agent · Cupertino & Palo Alto
The question I get most is "when should I apply for pre-approval?" The honest answer is: it depends on where your credit profile is today, and how far out your close date is. Those two variables determine whether you should be aggressively improving your score right now, or freezing your profile completely to protect it.

If you're 6 or more months from your target close date, this is your window. You have time to move the utilization needle, let disputes resolve, and see two or three billing cycles improve your average balance. Sharon tells clients in this window to treat credit improvement like a sprint with a known end date: set the target score, work backwards, and hit the specific paydown numbers that will get you there. A lender who runs a baseline soft-pull now can tell you exactly which account to target.

If you're inside 60 days from a planned offer, the calculus flips entirely. Now the priority is protecting what you have, not chasing points. That means: no new credit card applications, no car loans, no furniture financing, no co-signing anything for family. Every new account does two things that hurt a close: it adds a hard inquiry (immediate score drop of 5–10 points) and it lowers your average account age. Sharon has seen a $3.1M Palo Alto transaction go sideways because the buyer leased a car two weeks before their final approval — the new hard pull and monthly obligation pushed their DTI over the lender's threshold.

There's a third scenario that's less discussed: if you're already above 760, stop optimizing. Sharon's seen clients open new cards to get utilization "even lower," triggering hard pulls that moved a 762 down to 754 and dropped them out of the best rate tier. Above 760, the marginal gain from further credit optimization is zero. Freeze the profile, start focusing on documentation — tax returns, RSU vesting schedules, stock account statements.

For Marcus specifically — you're targeting a July close, which puts you roughly 5 months out from offer and 6 months out from close. That's the sweet spot: enough time to do one targeted paydown, let it score, and still have a stable profile by the time the lender runs their final credit pull 72 hours before closing. The playbook is clear. Don't open anything new. Pay down the highest-utilization card. Get a baseline soft-pull from a trusted lender — the one Sharon works with in Sunnyvale won't ding your score and will give you a precise score-to-rate projection.
